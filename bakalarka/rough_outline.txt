1) the notion of the euclidean tree is clear, just one note -- this has to be
    defined as an vertext-wise immersion of a regular graph theoretic tree
    into a Euclidean space. This is important in order to allow for overlaping
    vertices while not coalescing their identities.
2) then we have the notion of relaxed euclidean tree -- it is an edge-valued
    euclidean tree where the value of each edge is greater or equal to its
    euclidean length.
    Price of such a tree is the sum of all edge-values over all edges.
    This edge-valuation can be seen as another, concurent information about
    lengths of edges -- kind of _imposed_ edge lengths.
    Hence every relaxed euclidean tree has one center with respect to its 
    natural edge-lengths and another (possibly the same) with respect to 
    the imposed edge lengths.
3) A relaxed tree is said to be LED if, with respect to the imposed
    edge-lengths all leaves have the same excentricity.
   For every euclidean tree there are infinitely many valuations turning it
    into a LED tree.
    These valuations form a convex polytope and the price is a linear
    functional over this set.
    Hence there exist a unique minimal price for every given euclidean tree.
    We can call it LED-price of a euclidean tree.
    In this view every euclidean tree has a natural price given as a sum of
    edge-lengths over all edges and the LED-price.
    Optimisation of so defined prices over all possibe topologies and
    possitions of non-leaves is the euclidean steiner tree problem in the
    case of the native price, and the euclidean steiner led-tree problem in
    case of the LED-price.
4) Our aim (or one of our aims) is to solve completely the euclidean steiner
    led tree problem in cse of three leaves.
5) For every euclidean tree its LED-price and its center can be computed by a
    simple algorithm (this should be included in the theses)
6) In the case of three leaves every tree will have exactly one steiner
    vertex $S$.
    Solving the euclidean steiner led-tree problem ammounts to finding the
    optimal position of this $S$.
    So far this is done by a series of results that incrementaly uncovers the
    structure of the problem.
6a) Three given leaves always form a triangle, albeit sometimes a degenerate
    one.
    Obviously, our problem is equivariant wrt to all conformalities of the
    plane, so instances of this problem naturally decompose into classes of
    equivalence.
    These are in one-to-one correspondence with equivalence classes of the
    triangles in plane.
    Every such class has a unique representative with vertices at $(0,0)$ and
    $(1,0)$ and with the third vertex above the $x$-axis to the left of the
    $x-0.5$ axis with the side incident with $x$-axis being its longes side.
6a) First of all, it is relatively simple to prove, that the optimal LED-price
    is attained when $S$ lies on one of the axes of symmetry of the three
    sides of the triangle gien by the three leaves.
    This is true even in all the degenerate cases.
