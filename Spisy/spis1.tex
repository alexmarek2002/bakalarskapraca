	\section*{Abstract}
	\noindent \textbf{Title:} Shortest Euclidean LED-tree Problem as a Variant of the Shortest Euclidean Steiner Tree Problem\\
	\textbf{Abstract:} 
	This thesis studies minimal relaxed LED trees with three leaves and compares them with minimal Steiner trees on three terminals. The work first introduces basic notions from graph theory, Euclidean geometry, and optimization. It then summarizes the classical theory of Steiner trees, including their topology, geometric properties, and the Fermat's problem. The main part of the thesis focuses on relaxed LED trees and describes their behavior in the case of three leaves. A constructive procedure for obtaining a minimal relaxed LED tree is presented. The final part compares the obtained properties with the corresponding properties of Steiner minimal trees and shows the main geometric differences between these two optimization problems.
	
	\vspace{5pt}
	
	\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Steiner tree, LED tree, relaxed LED tree, Fermat's problem

	\chapter{Introduction}
	Optimization problems on graphs and geometric networks arise naturally in many areas of mathematics, computer science, engineering. One classical example is the Euclidean Steiner problem, whose objective is to connect a given set of terminals by a network of minimal total length while allowing the introduction of additional points, called Steiner points. Although the problem has a simple geometric formulation, it possesses a rich mathematical structure and has been studied extensively for many years.
	
	Recently, new optimization models similar to Steiner trees have been introduced. One such model is given by LED trees, which have a combinatorial structure similar to Steiner minimal trees but differ in several geometric properties. Since the theory of LED trees is relatively new, many of their structural and geometric properties are still not fully studied.
	
	The main focus of this thesis is the study of minimal relaxed LED trees in the case of three leaves and their comparison with minimal Steiner trees on three terminals. To provide the necessary background, the thesis first introduces basic notions from graph theory and Euclidean geometry. The work also summarizes the classical theory of Steiner trees, including their topology, geometric properties, and the special case of Fermat's problem.
	
	The central part of the thesis is devoted to the geometric analysis of relaxed LED trees with three leaves. We investigate the structure of minimal configurations and present a constructive procedure for obtaining minimal relaxed LED trees in this setting. The final part of the thesis compares the obtained results with the corresponding properties of Steiner minimal trees. Although both optimization problems are structurally similar, their geometric behavior differs in several important aspects.

	\section{Introduction to graph theory}
	Graph theory is considered a relatively young mathematical discipline. Its official beginning is often associated with the first monograph on graph theory, written by Dénes Kőnig in 1936. However, the origins of graph theory date 200 year back, when in 1736 Leonhard Euler published paper, that is regarded as the first paper in the history of graph theory. In this paper, Euler addressed the famous problem of walking through all the bridges of the city of Königsberg without crossing any bridge more than once. Euler represented the map of city and its bridges as a graph, topology and theory that he had done has shown it's importance much later, in various fields, including for example traffic planning, computer science or electrical engineering. More importantly it laid the groundwork for a new branch of mathematics, graph theory.
	
	From the perspective of graph theory, a graph is a mathematical structure consisting of a set of objects (vertices) and connections (edges) between these objects.
	Graphs can represent various real-world structures, from maps of cities connected by roads to electrical networks connecting sockets.
	Formally, a graph is defined as ordered pair \( G = (V, E) \), where \( V \) is a set of vertices, and \( E \subseteq \{\{v_i, v_j\} : v_i, v_j \in V\} \) is a set of edges, pairs of vertices.
	
	If we take a subset of the vertices of given graph, along with some or all of the edges between them we get subgraph. A subgraph of a graph \( G = (V, E) \) is a graph \( G' = (V', E') \) such that  
	\[
	V' \subseteq V \quad, \quad E' \subseteq E \cap \left\{ \{u,v\} \mid u,v \in V' \right\}.
	\]
	
	Edges in a graph can be either directed or undirected. A graph with directed edges is called a directed graph. In such a graph, each edge has an assigned direction from one vertex to another, we will not go into detail, as all graphs considered in this work are undirected. In an undirected graph, all edges are unordered pairs of vertices, undirected edges, representing symmetric relationships. Unless stated otherwise, consider all graphs in this work undirected.
	To understand next basic property of graphs, connectivity, we need to define term path. A path in a graph is a finite or infinite sequence of edges which joins a sequence of vertices, all of which are distinct. A graph is said to be connected if there exists a path between every pair of vertices. In contrast, a disconnected graph contains at least two vertices between which no path exists. This means the graph consists of two or more separate parts, called connected components. 
	We will refer to two vertices connected by an edge as adjacent, and the degree of a vertex is defined as the number of vertices adjacent to it(i.e., number of times it occurs in set of edges). In this context, a path can also be seen as a sequence of adjacent vertices, each of which appears at most once.
	
	\section{Weighted graphs}	
	In many applications, it is useful to associate a numerical value, called a weight, with each edge of a graph. For example, when modeling traffic, the vertices may represent cities and the edges represent roads, while the weight corresponds to the time required to travel from one city to another. 
	A weighted graph is a triple \( G = (V, E, w) \), where \( (V, E) \) is a graph and \( w : E \to \mathbb{R} \) is a weight function that assigns a real number to each edge. 
	In such graph, distance between two vertices \( u, v \in V \), denoted \( d_G(u,v) \),  is defined as the length of the shortest path between \( u \) and \( v \) in \( G \), it is computed as sum of the weights of edges along that path. Let us mention that if the graph is not weighted then usually distance is computed in the same way, with the only difference being that each edge is considered to have weight 1, i.e., \( d_G(u,v) \) equals the number of edges in the path.
	Based on the notion of distance, we define the following related terms: eccentricity, radius, and center of a graph. The eccentricity of a vertex is the greatest distance from that vertex to any other vertex in the graph. The radius of the graph is the smallest eccentricity among all vertices. The center of the graph is the set of all vertices whose eccentricity is equal to the radius.
	
	Another example, perhaps the most intuitive one, of a structure that can be represented by a weighted graph is network of points in a plane or space. Such a structure is called Euclidean graph or geometric graph.
	A Euclidean graph is defined as \( G = (V, E, \mu, w) \), where:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \( (V, E) \) is a graph,
		\item \( \mu : V \to \mathbb{E}^n \) is a mapping that assigns to each vertex a position in n-dimensional Euclidean space,
		\item \( w : E \to \mathbb{R} \) is a weight function.
	\end{itemize}
	The weight of an edge \( \{u,v\} \in E \) is given by the Euclidean distance between the images of its endpoints:
	\[
	w(\{u,v\}) = |\mu(u) - \mu(v)| = \sqrt{ \sum_{i=1}^{n} (\mu(u)_i - \mu(v)_i)^2 }.
	\]
	
	\section{Trees}
	In this thesis we will particularly focus on a special type of graph, a tree.
	Tree is a connected graph with no cycle.
	Term tree was first used by the English mathematician Arthur Cayley, who applied it to count the number of different types of alkanes. The fusion of mathematical and chemical research later led to the establishment of standard terminology in graph theory.
	
	Consider we have finite connected graph G(V, E), then a subgraph of G(V, E) which contains all the vertices of G and is a tree, this subgraph is called a spanning tree. If additionally each edge of G has a known weight(length) \(w(e)>0\), than the spanning tree of G with the minimal sum of the weights of the edges, is called minimum spanning tree. The importance of minimum spanning trees is evident. For example, if we aim to build railway connections between a set of cities at minimal cost. We can model the cities and all possible connections as a graph, and then find a minimum spanning tree of this graph to determine the most efficient network. 
	The minimal spanning tree problem is formulated as follows: Find the spanning tree, G'(V,E'), for which the sum of the weights of the edges \( \sum_{e \in E'} w(e)\) is a minimum. Two most common algorithms used to solve the minimum spanning tree problem are Prim's algorithm and Kruskal's algorithm.
	
	An analogy to a spanning tree in the Euclidean setting is the Euclidean spanning tree. A Euclidean tree is a Euclidean graph that is a tree, hence is connected and has no cycle. The term spanning indicates that the tree includes all the vertices of the original graph. Minimum spanning tree problem becomes Euclidean minimum spanning tree problem and is: Find the Euclidean spanning tree for which the sum of the Euclidean distances between n points connected by edges is a minimum.   
	
	\section{Euclidean Steiner Problem}
	If we would continue with our example about railway connection, it would naturally appear that a more effective option is possible, to connect cities with less amount of rails. If we ought add transport hubs between cities in favorable positions, we would might be able to lower construction costs but still connect all cities. This is where Steiner trees emerge. They are consequence of efforts to even lower the length of (euclidean) minimal spanning tree, by allowing to add additional points, to the set of current points. We call this task Euclidean Steiner problem. 
	Euclidean Steiner problem, in form that it is now, was first introduced by Courant and Robbins in 1941, in their book What is Mathematics?. In 1968 Gilbert and Pollak named solution for this problem, Steiner minimal trees, in their paper Steiner minimal trees. These terms were called after Jacob Steiner (1796-1863), who was professor specializing in geometry, at University of Berlin. Steiner has contibution in this area geometry, he worked on problems similar to this one but it in not clear if he was aware of this exact version of the problem.
	Euclidean Steiner problem is a modification to euclidean minimum spanning tree problem. In Euclidean minimal spanning tree problem on graph \( G = (V, E, \mu, w) \), every point \(v_i\) must be contained in the subgraph of solution of the problem. On the other hand in Euclidean Steiner problem we can add to the set \(V\) additional points. To distinguish points given and added, we call the given set of points terminals \(t_i\) and added points Steiner points \(s_i\).
	\[
	\begin{aligned}
		T &= \{t_1, \dots, t_n\}, \\
		S &= \{s_1, \dots, s_m\}
	\end{aligned}
	\]
	
	An Euclidean Steiner tree for a set of terminals \( T \) is a Euclidean tree
	\[
	G = (V, E, \mu, w),
	\]
	where
	\[
	\begin{aligned}
	V &= T \cup S \\
	 \mu &: V \to \mathbb{E}^d  
	\end{aligned}
	\]
	and the weight function
	\[ w : E \to \mathbb{R} \]
	 is Euclidean distance. Also since the Euclidean metric is used, every edge of a Steiner minimal tree is represented by a straight line segment.
	 
	 The Euclidean Steiner tree problem consists of finding a set of \(S\) in Euclidean space, and a tree \( G = (T \cup S, E,\mu, w) \) such that the total length
	 \[
	 \sum_{e \in E} w(e)
	 \]
	 is minimal.
	 
	 We have settled terms: Euclidean Steiner problem, Steiner tree, minimal Steiner tree. We will spend few following lines describing properties of Steiner trees and it's nature. This should be understood as insight to the topic rather than rigorous mathematical text. We briefly present the theory from \cite{soothill2010} and state established observations without proving them. Our main focus will than be on Steiner tree with three terminals.

	Relatively minimal tree for a topology is a tree that is the shortest among the trees with equivalent topology.
	
	Number of Steiner points is next important structural property. If we consider our efforts of minimizing tree, it occurs that there is upper limit on how many Steiner points we can add. The reason why this is the case will be partially clarified later.
	A Steiner tree has at most \(m\) Steiner points
	\[
	m \le n-2.
	\]
	,where n is the number of terminals.
	
	Also there is name for the case when
	\[
	m = n-2
	\]
	such tree is called a full Steiner tree.
	Moreover, in such topology every Steiner point has degree three and every terminal has degree one, or in other words, every terminal is a leaf.
	
	The Euclidean Steiner problem is computationally difficult and belongs to the class of NP-hard optimization problems. In the Euclidean Steiner problem, terminals are usually understood as the input, while Steiner points are treated as variables. On behalf of the Steiner points there is enormous variability in how the final tree can be constructed. The first source of variability is the number of Steiner points introduced, the second is the way in which they are connected within the tree, and the third is their placement in the Euclidean plane. As a consequence, the number of possible Steiner tree configurations grows superexponentially with the number of terminals. 
	
	We now state several additional properties of Steiner minimal trees. Some of them will be justified immediately, while others will become clearer later in the discussion of the three-terminal case.
	
	A fundamental local optimality condition is that no two incident edges of a Steiner minimal tree may meet at an angle smaller than \(120^\circ\). This property plays a central role in the structure of Steiner trees.
	
	An immediate consequence is that Steiner minimal trees contain no crossing edges. Indeed, if two edges intersected, at least one of the angles formed at the intersection would necessarily be smaller than \(120^\circ\), contradicting the optimality condition. Moreover, such a crossing could always be replaced by a shorter non-crossing configuration.
	
	Another important structural property concerns the degrees of Steiner points. A Steiner point cannot have degree one, since such a point would only extend the tree without contributing to connectivity and could therefore be removed to shorten the total length of the tree. Similarly, a Steiner point cannot have degree two, because the two adjacent edges could be replaced by a single edge connecting their endpoints directly, again without increasing the total length.
	
	On the other hand, the \(120^\circ\) angle condition also excludes Steiner points of degree four or higher. Around a single point, it is impossible for four or more edges to meet while maintaining angles of at least \(120^\circ\). Consequently, every Steiner point in a Steiner minimal tree has degree exactly three.
	
	\section{Rooted trees}

	Another special type of tree is a rooted tree. A rooted tree is a tree
	\[
	G = (V,E)
	\]
	in which one vertex \( R \in V \) is distinguished and called the root.
	
	A Euclidean rooted tree is a Euclidean tree
	\[
	G = (V,E,\mu,w)
	\]
	together with a distinguished root vertex \( R \in V \). The image
	\[
	\mu(R) \in \mathbb{E}^n
	\]
	is called the root of the Euclidean rooted tree.
	
	In the following, we introduce the basic terminology and notions related to rooted trees
	\begin{itemize}
		\item A \emph{leaf} of a tree is any vertex of degree one (with just one adjacent edge). Any other vertex is called an \emph{inner vertex}.
		\item The \emph{root path} of a vertex of a rooted tree is the unique path that connects the vertex with the root.
		\item The \emph{depth} of a vertex in a rooted tree is the length of its root path. In the non-Euclidean case, this is simply the number of edges in the path, while in the Euclidean case, the depth is the standard Euclidean length of the path.
		\item A \emph{leaf path} is any downward path that connects a vertex with a leaf. A \emph{downward path} is any path where each vertex has a greater depth than its predecessor.
		\item The \emph{height} of a vertex is the length of its longest leaf path. The \emph{height of the tree} is defined as the height of the root.
		\item The \emph{parent} of a vertex is its successor on the root path. A \emph{child} of a vertex is its successor on any of its leaf paths.
	\end{itemize}
	
	Rooted trees are important for our purposes because LED trees are derived from them. They provide a basic structure on which additional properties of LED trees are built.
	
	\section{Relaxed LED trees}	
	
	Informally speaking, LED trees can be understood as rooted trees in which all root-to-leaf paths have the same length.
	
	In our setting, we additionally introduce the concept of relaxed LED trees. Informally, relaxation means that edges of the tree are no longer restricted to straight line segments. Geometrically, this can be imagined as replacing a straight edge by a curve connecting the same pair of vertices. Consequently, the length assigned to an edge may differ from the Euclidean distance between its endpoints.
	
	Relaxed price of an edge connecting vertices \(u\) and \(v\) will be denoted by \(\Price{u}{v}.\)

	
	For every edge connecting two vertices in a tree, we assign two values:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \( |uv| \): the Euclidean distance between the two vertices \( u \) and \( v \),
		\item \( \Price{u}{v} \): the relaxed (or curved) value of the edge between \( u \) and \( v \),
	\end{itemize}
	where \( |uv| \leq \Price{u}{v} \) for every edge.
	
	We will also denote \( \Price{u}{v} \) for vertices \( u \) and \( v \) that are not directly connected by an edge. In this case, \( \Price{u}{v} \) represents the sum of relaxed values along the unique path from \( u \) to \( v \). Since a tree contains no cycles, there exists exactly one such path for every pair of vertices.
	
	\begin{definition}\label{def:led}
		A tree is called a relaxed LED tree if the price of the path from the root \( R \) to each leaf is the same. That is, for any two leaves \( l_1 \) and \( l_2 \), it holds that
		\[
		\Price{R}{l_1} = \Price{R}{l_2}.
		\]
	\end{definition}
	
	In other words, for any two leaves \( l_1 \) and \( l_2 \):
	\[
	\sum_{(u,v) \in M_1} \Price{u}{v} = \sum_{(u,v) \in M_2} \Price{u}{v},
	\]
	where \( M_1 \) and \( M_2 \) are the sets of edges of paths from the root \( R \) to the leaves \( l_1 \) and \( l_2 \), respectively. The sums are taken over all edges \( (u,v) \) along the corresponding paths.
	
	From the definition of relaxed LED trees, we obtain a relation that we will find useful later.
	\begin{lemma}\label{lem:led}
		Let \( V \) be a vertex that is neither a leaf nor a root, and let \( L_1 \) and \( L_2 \) be leaves. Assume that \( V \) is a common ancestor of both leaves \( L_1 \) and \( L_2 \).
		If the root \( R \) does not lie on either of the paths from \( V \) to \( L_1 \) or from \( V \) to \( L_2 \), then:
		\[
		\Price{V}{L_1} = \Price{V}{L_2}.
		\]			
	\end{lemma}
	\begin{proof}
		Let \(N\) be the path consisting of edges \((v_i, v_{i+1})\), where \(v_0 = R\) and \(v_k = V\).
		Let \(M'_1\) be the path consisting of edges \((v_i, v_{i+1})\), where \(v_0 = V\) and \(v_k = L_1\).
		Let \(M'_2\) be the path consisting of edges \((v_i, v_{i+1})\), where \(v_0 = V\) and \( v_k = L_2\).
		Define \( M_1 = N \cup M'_1 \) and \( M_2 = N \cup M'_2 \).\\
		Since the prices along the paths from \( R \) to \( L_1 \) and \( R \) to \( L_2 \) are equal, we have:
		\begin{align*}
			\Price{R}{l_1} &= \Price{R}{l_2}\\
			\sum_{(u,v) \in M_1} \Price{u}{v} &= \sum_{(u,v) \in M_2} \Price{u}{v}\\
			\sum\!_{(v_i,v_{i+1}) \in N}\Price{v_i}{v_{i+1}} +\sum\!_{(v_i,v_{i+1}) \in M'_1}\Price{v_i}{v_{i+1}} 
			&=\\=\sum\!_{(v_i,v_{i+1}) \in N}\Price{v_i}{v_{i+1}}& +\sum\!_{(v_i,v_{i+1}) \in M'_2}\Price{v_i}{v_{i+1}}\\
			\Price{R}{V} + \Price{V}{l_1} &= \Price{R}{V} + \Price{V}{l_2} \\
			\Price{V}{l_1} &= \Price{V}{l_2}
		\end{align*}
		For clarity, we expanded the price terms into sums here.  
		However, in the rest of the text, we will write price values without explicitly unfolding the paths.
	\end{proof}
	
	We define the price of tree \(\Ptotal{T}\) as the sum of all individual \(\Price{}{}\) values.

	\section{Fermat's problem}
	
	Historically speaking, in section about Steiner Trees, we were getting ahead of ourself. Respecting the historical chronology, it must be noted that Fermat's problem was studied long before Steiner tree problem. Fermat's problem was called after Pierre de Fermat (1601-1655), French mathematician. Unlike Euclidean Steiner problem, Fermat's problem was formulated by Fermat. This is the proposed form: Given three points in a plane, find a fourth point such
	that the sum of its distances to the three given points is a minimum.
	In this form it's a case of Euclidean Steiner problem where:
	\[
	\begin{aligned}
		T &= \{t_1, \dots, t_n\}, n=3 \\
		S &= \{s_1\}
	\end{aligned}
	\]
	and
	\[
	\begin{aligned}
		V &= T \cup S \\
		\mu &: V \to \mathbb{E}^2  
	\end{aligned}
	\]
	
	In section about Steiner Trees we have already mentioned some properties about them. Not only that they apply for Fermat's problem as well, but proportion of them were obtained from case with tree terminals. Since complex structure of thee with many terminals, can be arranged as simple triangles with Steiner point inside. In fact minimal Steiner trees behave this way and we will later show this. We have introduced the definition and would like to now discuss Fermat problem further. Whenever the discussed results apply to the general case as well, we will point them out.  
	
	Approximately one hundred years after Fermat proposed the problem, two mathematicians, Torricelli and Simpson, introduced geometric constructions for its solution. These are purely geometric constructions in the Euclidean plane. The obtained solution corresponds to the minimal Steiner tree for three terminals. The constructions are illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:TorSim}.
	
	The Torricelli method begins by constructing an equilateral triangle on each side of the original triangle $ABC$. Subsequently, circumcircles of the constructed equilateral triangles are drawn. The Steiner point is then located at the common intersection of all three circumcircles.
	
	The Simpson method starts in a similar manner, again by constructing equilateral triangles on the sides of $ABC$. Afterward, for each constructed equilateral triangle, a line segment is drawn connecting the newly constructed point, which does not belong to the original triangle, with the point opposite to the side on which the corresponding equilateral triangle was constructed. The intersection of these three line segments determines the Steiner point.
	
	\begin{figure}[H]
		\centering
		
		\begin{subfigure}{0.48\textwidth}
			\centering
			\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/torricelli_method.pdf}
			\caption{Torricelli method}
			\label{fig:torricelli_method}
		\end{subfigure}
		\hfill
		\begin{subfigure}{0.48\textwidth}
			\centering
			\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/simpson_method.pdf}
			\caption{Simpson method}
			\label{fig:simpson_method}
		\end{subfigure}
		
		\caption{Geometric constructions of the Fermat point using the Torricelli and Simpson methods.}
		\label{fig:TorSim}
	\end{figure}

	It follows that both geometric constructions fail whenever one of the interior angles of triangle $\triangle ABC$ exceeds \(120^\circ\). In such a case, the constructed point lies outside the triangle, which contradicts the minimality property of the Steiner tree. This leads to the following characterization of the solution to Fermat's problem.
	
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item If one of the interior angles of $\triangle ABC$ is greater than or equal to \(120^\circ\), then the solution to Fermat's problem coincides with the point at which this angle occurs.
		
		\item If all interior angles of $\triangle ABC$ are smaller than \(120^\circ\), then the solution is the Torricelli point, equivalently the intersection point of the Simpson lines. In this case, the obtained point forms three angles of \(120^\circ\) with the points \(A\), \(B\), and \(C\).
	\end{enumerate}

	It should be noted that the characteristic \(120^\circ\) angle property of Steiner trees originates from three-terminal case. In the configuration described above, the tree edges incident to the Steiner point meet at angles of \(120^\circ\). This local optimality condition also appears in larger Steiner minimal trees and forms the basis for many structural properties and optimization rules related to Steiner trees.
	
	The three-terminal case also explains the angle condition stated in Section~\ref{sec:steiner_trees}. If two edges of a tree meet at an angle smaller than \(120^\circ\), the total length of the tree can be decreased by introducing an additional Steiner point in a suitable position. Consequently, such configurations cannot occur in a Steiner minimal tree.

	\begin{definition}\label{def:LED3}

Vertex \( S \) is adjacent to two leaves and to the \( R \), while the remaining leaf is adjacent directly to the root \( R \). Every edge has price value \(\Price{u}{v}; |uv| \leq \Price{u}{v} \) and according to \cref{def:led}:

 	\section{minimal relaxed LED tree with three leaves}	
 	
	Throughout this section, we work with the tree \(T\) as defined in \cref{def:LED3} our purpose will be, to analyze how to optimize positions of points \(S, R\), in order to achieve minimal total price \(\Ptotal{T}\) of three leaved tree, in particular topology. we assume the tree as defined in \cref{def:LED3}. 	\end{align}

	We know that we can relax edges to achieve \cref{lem:led}, even if euclidean distances from root to leafs are not equal, the first proposition is about how to compute \(\Ptotal{T}\) with minimal amount of relaxation. 
	\begin{proposition}\label{prop:tp} 
		Let us distinguish four mutually exclusive cases for fixed \(A,B,C,S,R\), depending on lengths of edges. Minimal prices of tree \(\Ptotal{T}\), for these cases are:
		\begin{enumerate}
			\item if \( |AS| > |BS|, |AS|+|SR| \geq |CR| \); then \(\Ptotal{T} = 2 |SR| + 3 |SA|\)
			\item if \( |AS| < |BS|, |BS|+|SR| \geq |CR| \); then \(\Ptotal{T} = 2 |SR| + 3 |SB|\)
			\item if \( |AS| > |BS|, |AS|+|SR| \leq |CR| \); then \(\Ptotal{T} = |SA| + 2 |CR|\)
			\item if \( |AS| < |BS|, |BS|+|SR| \leq |CR| \); then \(\Ptotal{T} = |SB| + 2 |CR|\)
		\end{enumerate}
	\end{proposition}
	\begin{proof}
		The second case is analogous to the first one, and the fourth case is analogous to the third. Therefore, we only prove the first and third cases.
		
		By Lemma~\ref{lem:led}, we know that in all cases \( \Price{S}{A} = \Price{S}{B} \).
		We also know that \( |UV| \leq  $ \Price{U}{V}$ \), hence the smallest possible value for \Price{S}{A} is \(|SA|\) and for \Price{S}{R} is \(|SR|\).
		\begin{align*}	
			\Price{S}{A} = \Price{S}{B} &= |SA| \\
			\Price{S}{R} &= |SR| \\
			\Price{R}{C} = \Price{R}{S} + \Price{S}{A} &= |SA| + |SR|
		\end{align*}
		The total prize of three is now computed as follows:
		\begin{align*}	
			\Ptotal{T} &= \Price{S}{A} + \Price{S}{B} + \Price{C}{R} + \Price{R}{S} \\
			&= |SA| + |SA| + |SR| + |SA| + |SR| \\
			&= 2 |SR| + 3 |SA|
		\end{align*}
		
		Let us analyze the third case where \( |AS| > |BS|, |AS|+|SR| \leq |CR| \).
		We know that:
		\begin{align*}
			\Price{S}{A} = \Price{S}{B}
		\end{align*}
		also that smallest possible value for \Price{S}{A} is \(|SA|\).
		\begin{align*}	
			\Price{S}{A} = \Price{S}{B} = |SA| \\
		\end{align*}
		since \(|CR| \geq |AS|+|SR| > |BS|+|SR| \), the smallest possible value for \Price{R}{C} is \(|CR|\)
		\begin{align*}
			\Price{R}{C} &= \Price{R}{A} = \Price{R}{B} = |CR| \\
			\Price{R}{C} &= \Price{R}{S} + \Price{S}{A} \\
			\Price{R}{S} &= |CR| - |AS|
		\end{align*}
		The total prize of three is then computed as follows:
		\begin{align*}	
			\Ptotal{T} &= \Price{S}{A} + \Price{S}{B} + \Price{C}{R} + \Price{R}{S} \\
			&= |SA| + |SA| + |CR| + |CR| - |SA| \\
			&= |SA| + 2 |CR|
		\end{align*}
	\hfill $\square$
	\end{proof}

	We will now analyze the relation between point S and axis of the \(AB\), we preserve the connectivity from previous example.
	\begin{proposition}\label{prop:Sino}
		Let points \(A,B,C,R\) be fixed. Let \(o\) be a perpendicular bisector of the segment \(AB\). 
		If the total price \(P(T)\) of the tree is minimal, then the point \(S\) lies on axis \(o\).
	\end{proposition}	
	\begin{proof}
		Suppose, that \(S\) does not lie on \(o\) \(|AS|\neq |BS|\). Without loss of generality, we may assume that
		\[
		|AS|>|BS|.
		\]
		this means that axis \(o\) intersect segment \(AS\). 
		Now Consider the ellipse
		\[
		E=\{X\in\mathbb{R}^2:\ |AX|+|XR|=|AS|+|SR|\}.
		\]
		This ellipse has foci \(A\) and \(R\), and it passes through the point \(S\).
		Since the segment \(AS\) lies inside the region bounded by \(E\), and since
		\(o\) intersects the segment \(AS\), the axis \(o\) also intersects the
		interior of the region bounded by \(E\). Furthermore, \(o\) intersects the
		ellipse \(E\) in two points.
		We choose the point for which \(|AQ|<|AS|.\)
		
		Since \(Q\in E\), by the definition of the ellipse we have
		\[
		|AQ|+|QR|=|AS|+|SR|.
		\]
		So total price of the tree is still computed according to the same pricing rule from proposition\ref{prop:tp} as before. Regardless of which of the possible pricing rules applies, the total price decreases after replacing \(S\) by \(Q\).
		Since \(Q\in o\), we have
		\[
		|AQ|=|BQ|.
		\]
		Thus, moving \(S\) to the new position \(Q\) gives a tree with a smaller total price. Therefore, \(S\) must lie on the perpendicular bisector \(o\) of the segment \(AB\).
	\end{proof}	 
	
	Knowing that in a minimal tree the vertex \(S\) lies on the perpendicular
	bisector \(o\) of the segment \(AB\), we now analyze how the root \(R\) should
	be positioned in order to minimize the total price.
	
	For simplicity, we will occasionally use the term \emph{axis} instead of
	\emph{perpendicular bisector}; both terms will refer to the same object.
	
	\begin{proposition}\label{prop:R_on_SC}
		Let points \(A,B,C,S\) be fixed. If the total price \(\Ptotal{T}\) is minimal, then the vertex \(R\)
		lies on the edge \(SC\).
	\end{proposition}
	
	\begin{proof}
		Without loss of generality, assume that \(|AS|\geq |BS|\) ans Suppose that
		\(R\) does not lie on the line \(SC\).
		
		Let \(H\) be the branch of the hyperbola with foci \(S\) and \(C\) passing
		through \(R\). Thus, for every point \(X\in H\), the value
		\[
		|XC|-|XS|
		\]
		is constant. Since every brach of hyperbola intersects segment between it's foci, the branch \(H\) intersects the segment
		\(SC\), We denote this point \(Q\).
		
		By the definition of \(H\), we have
		\[
		|QC|-|QS| = |RC|-|RS|
		\]
		According to \cref{prop:tp}, the rule determining the optimal total
		price depends only on the comparison between \(|AS|+|SR|\) and \(|CR|\),
		equivalently on comparison of \(|CR|-|SR|\) and \(|AS|\). Since this value is constant on
		the hyperbola branch \(H\), replacing \(R\) by \(Q\) does not change the pricing rule.
		
		Moreover, since \(Q\in SC\), it follows that
		\[
		|QC|+|QS| = |SC|
		\]
		and, because \(R\notin SC\), the triangle inequality gives
		\[
		|RC|+|RS| > |SC|
		\]
		Combining these relations with the equality of the differences, we obtain
		\[
		|QC|<|RC|
		\qquad\text{and}\qquad
		|QS|<|RS|.
		\]	
		However, in both possible pricing cases, the total price is a positive linear
		combination of the distances from \(R\) to the foci \(S\)
		and \(C\). After replacing \(R\) by \(Q\), both relevant distances decrease.
		Hence \(R\) must lie on edge \(SC\).
	\end{proof}

	The following proposition is established under the assumptions of \cref{prop:R_on_SC,prop:Sino}.
	\begin{proposition}\label{PbasedonR}
		Let points \(A,B,C,S\) be fixed. If the total price \(\Ptotal{T}\) is minimal, then we have two cases regarding the position of vertex \(R\):
		\begin{enumerate}
			\item If \(|AS| \geq |CS|\), then the minimal total price \(\Ptotal{T}\) is attained when \(S = R\) and it is \(\Ptotal{T} = 3|AS|.\)
			\item If \(|AS| < |CS|\), then the minimal total price \(\Ptotal{T}\) is attained when \(|AS| + |SR| = |RC|\) and it is \(\Ptotal{T} = 2|AS| + |CS|.\)
		\end{enumerate}
	\end{proposition}
	\begin{proof}
		Let \(V(R)\) be a function derived from Proposition\ref{prop:tp}, from comparison of the expressions \(|AS|+|SR|\) and \(|CR|\) in second term. 
		We study the behavior of the function
		\[
		V(R) := |AS| + |SR| - |RC|
		\]
		as the point \(R\) moves along the line \(SC\).
		The function \(V\) is continuous, since it is a sum of three continuous
		functions. If the point \(R\) lies outside the segment \(SC\), then its
		distances to both \(S\) and \(C\) change equally, and hence the value
		of \(V(R)\) remains constant outside the segment \(SC\).
		If \(R\) lies on the segment \(SC\) and moves towards the leaf \(C\),
		the term \(|SR|\) increases while the term \(|RC|\) decreases. Therefore,
		\(V(R)\) increases monotonically along the segment \(SC\) in the direction
		from \(S\) to \(C\).
		we conclude that \(V\) attains its
		minimum at the point \(S\): \(V(S) = |AS| - |SC|\) and its maximum at the point \(C\): \(V(C) = |AS| + |SC|\).
		According to \cref{prop:tp}, the rule for computing the minimal
		total price of the LED tree depends on the sign of \(V(R)\).
		Indeed, if \(V(R) \geq 0\), then
		\[
		|AS| + |SR| \geq |RC|,
		\]
		and the total price is computed according to cases (1) or (2).
		Otherwise, the total price is computed according to cases (3) or (4).
		Note that at the point \(C\), the value \(V(C)\) is always nonnegative.
		However, at the point \(S\), the sign of \(V(S)\) depends on the geometry
		of the tree.
		
		For \(|AS| \geq |CS|\) the minimal possible value of \(V\) is in \(S\) and it is always nonnegative, hence it is nonnegative on entire segment \(SC\).
		Therefore, regardless of the position of \(R\) on the line \(SC\), the total
		price of the tree is computed as \(\Ptotal{T} = 3|SA| + 2|SR|\). This expression is minimized when \(R=S\). Therefore the total price is
		\[
		\Ptotal{T} = 3|SA|.
		\]
		The edge \(RC\) is relaxed, except for \(|AS| = |CS|\). 
		
		Now consider case \(|AS| < |CS|\). In this case value of \(V\) in \(S\) is:
		\[
		V(S)=|AS|-|SC|<0,
		\] and in \(C\) is \[
		V(C)=|AS|+|SC|>0.
		\] 
		Since \(V\) is continuous and monotone on the segment \(SC\), it follows that there exists a unique point \(Q\in SC\) such that
		\[
		V(Q)=0.
		\]
		It is clear that the segment \(QS\) is the region on which \(V\) is negative, therefore in this region the total price is 
		\[
		\Ptotal{T} = |SA| + 2|RC|.
		\]
		If the point \(R\) moves along this segment towards the point \(Q\), it also
		approaches the leaf \(C\), and thus	the total price decreases monotonically.
		
		Similarly, the segment \(QC\) is the region on which \(V\) is positive. Hence, in this region  the total price is 
		\[
		\Ptotal{T} = 3|SA| + 2|SR|.
		\]
		If the point \(R\) moves along this ray towards \(Q\), it also approaches
		the vertex \(S\), and therefore the	total price again decreases monotonically.
		
		Consequently, regardless of whether \(R\) lies in the region where \(V\) is
		nonnegative or negative, the total price decreases as \(R\) approaches \(Q\).
		It follows that the minimal total price is attained at \(R = Q\).
		
		Since \(Q\) is the zero of \(V\), we have
		\begin{align*}
		V(R) &= 0 \\
		|AS| + |SR| - |RC| &= 0\\
		|AS| + |SR| &= |RC|\\
		\end{align*}
		Now we show that, under the condition \(|AS| + |SR| = |RC|\), the pricing rule corresponding to both cases (1), (2) and cases (3), (4)
		coincides. Moreover, if \(|AS| = |BS|\), then the total price of the tree is given simply by the sum of the Euclidean lengths of its edges. In the computation, we also use \cref{cor:sr}.
		\begin{align*}
			\Ptotal{T} &= |AS| + 2|RC| \\
			 &= |AS| + 2 (|AS| + |SR|) \\
			 &= 3|AS| + 2|SR| \\ 
			 &= 3|AS| + 2\frac{|CS| - |AS|}{2} \\
			 &= 2|AS| + |CS|\\
			 &= |AS| + |BS| + |CS| \\
		\end{align*}
	\end{proof}
	
	
	\begin{corollary}\label{cor:sr}
	If \(|AS| < |CS|\), to minimize the total length of the LED tree, the root \( R \) should be placed on edge \(CS\) such that:
	  \[
	  |SR| = \frac{|CS| - |AS|}{2}.
	  \]
	\end{corollary}
	\begin{proof}
		We know that \(|CS| = |SR| + |RC|\).
		From the \( |AS|+|SR| = |CR|  \), we derive:
		\begin{align*}
			|AS| + |SR| &= |CS| - |SR| \\
			|SR| &= \frac{|CS| - |AS|}{2} \\
			\Rightarrow \quad |RC| &= |CS| - |SR| = |CS| - \frac{|CS| + |AS|}{2}
		\end{align*}
	\end{proof}

		\begin{proposition}\label{prop:xSopt}
			Let \(a,c,\ell>0\) be given. Let us define the function \(f(x)\) that will represent euclidean length of minimal relaxed LED tree 
			\[
			f(x) := 2\sqrt{a^{2}+x^{2}}+\sqrt{c^{2}+(\ell-x)^{2}} .
			\]
			Then the optimal position of the point \(S\) along the axis is obtained by
			minimizing \(f\). Moreover, every interior minimizer \(x\) satisfies
			\begin{equation}\label{eq:xSpos}
				3x^{4} - 6\ell x^{3}
				+ \left(4c^{2} + 3\ell^{2} - a^{2}\right)x^{2}
				+ 2\ell a^{2}x
				- \ell^{2}a^{2}
				= 0 .
			\end{equation}
		\end{proposition}
	 	\begin{proof}
		 By the previous results, in the optimal configuration of \(R\), the total price of the tree reduces to the sum of the Euclidean lengths
		 \[
		 |AS|+|BS|+|CS|.
		 \]
		 Since \(S\) lies on the perpendicular bisector of \(AB\), we have
		 \[
		 |AS|=|BS|.
		 \]
		 Hence the total price can be written as
		 \[
		 2|AS|+|CS|.
		 \]
		
		We now express \(f\) according to Figure~\ref{fig:Sopt}, using Pythagoras theorem. 
		\begin{align*}
			f
			&= 2|AS| + |CS| \\
			&= 2\sqrt{a^{2} + x^{2}} + \sqrt{c^{2} + (\ell - x)^{2}} ,
		\end{align*}
		
		where \(a\), \(c\), and \(\ell\) are fixed, and variable \(x\) represents the position of the Steiner point \(S\) along the symmetry axis.
		Since the length tree \(f(x)\) is expressed as a differentiable function of the variable \(x\), its minimum is attained at a critical point. We therefore compute the derivative of \(f(x)\) and set it equal to zero.
		\begin{align*}
			\frac{\mathrm{d}f}{\mathrm{d}x}
			&= \frac{2x}{\sqrt{a^{2} + x^{2}}}
			- \frac{\ell - x}{\sqrt{c^{2} + (\ell - x)^{2}}} =0 
		\end{align*}
		\begin{equation}\label{eq:xSpos}
			3x^{4} - 6\ell x^{3}
			+ \left(4c^{2} + 3\ell^{2} - a^{2}\right)x^{2}
			+ 2\ell a^{2}x
			- \ell^{2}a^{2}
			= 0 .
		\end{equation}
		To justify that the critical point corresponds to a minimum, we note that the function \(f\) is convex, since each term is a convex function of \(x\).
		Therefore, any critical point of \(f\) is a global minimizer.
 		\end{proof}
 
 	
	
	\begin{remark}\label{Snell}
	We would to use the opportunity of relation expressed above to point out analogy to physics, namely with Snell's law from geometrical optics. Snell's law describes the path of light passing through two different media. A classical illustration is the problem of a lifeguard trying to reach a drowning person as quickly as possible: since the speed of motion on the beach differs from the speed in water, the time-minimizing path is not a straight line, but a broken one.
	
	Snell's law is given by
	\[
	\frac{\sin \theta_1}{v_1}=\frac{\sin \theta_2}{v_2},
	\]
	or equivalently,
	\[
	n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2,
	\]
	where \(v_1, v_2\) denote the propagation speeds in the two media, \(n_1, n_2\) are the corresponding refractive indices, and \(\theta_1, \theta_2\) between normal and light.

		Snell law can be derived in various ways. We find particularly similar Derivation from Fermat's principle. Which states that light travels the path which takes the least time.
		It consist of taking the derivative of the optical path length. Found stationary point defines the path:
		\[
		T(x)=\frac{\sqrt{x^2+a^2}}{v_1}+\frac{\sqrt{b^2+(\ell-x)^2}}{v_2}.
		\]
		\[
		\frac{\mathrm d T}{\mathrm d x}
		=
		\frac{x}{v_1\sqrt{x^2+a^2}}
		-
		\frac{\ell-x}{v_2\sqrt{(\ell-x)^2+b^2}}.
		\]
		At a stationary point, we therefore obtain
		\[
		\frac{x}{v_1\sqrt{x^2+a^2}}
		=
		\frac{\ell-x}{v_2\sqrt{(\ell-x)^2+b^2}}.
		\]
		Since
		\[
		\sin\theta_1=\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+a^2}},
		\qquad
		\sin\theta_2=\frac{\ell-x}{\sqrt{(\ell-x)^2+b^2}},
		\]
		it follows that
		\[
		\frac{\sin\theta_1}{v_1}=\frac{\sin\theta_2}{v_2},
		\]
		In our setting of finding minimal S for configuration of tree points, this relation takes the form
		\[
		2 \sin \theta_1 = \sin \theta_2.
		\]
		which represents a weighted analogue of Snell's law. Where the weights reflect the fact that the contribution of the branch \(AS\) appears twice in function \(L(T)=2|AS|+|CS|\).
	\end{remark}

	From the results of the previous proofs, we can conclude that the only way to construct a non-relaxed tree for \( C \in R_1 \) is to impose the condition \(|SA| = |SB| = |SC|.\) Consequently, the point \( S \) is the circumcenter of triangle \( ABC \). It is clear that this circumcenter lies below the segment \( AB \). We denote it's vertical distance by \( d \).	For this configuration, the total tree length \(P(T)\) can be expressed as \(g=3|SA|\). Observe that as the distance \(d\) decreases, the value of \(g\) also decreases. This implies that \(|SA|>|SC|\),therefore the edge \(CS\) becomes relaxed.
	Since \(g\) depends only on \(|SA|\), and the distance \(|SA|\) is minimized precisely when the segment \(SA\) is perpendicular to the line \(AB\), it follows that the minimal value of \(g\) is attained at the point \(S=M\).
	
	For the reason to distinguish the relaxed and non-relaxed configurations functions, we denote that \(\Ptotal{T}\) is equal to function \(h=3|SA|\). Even though \(h\)and\(g\) coincides.
	\end{proof}
	
	\section{Selection of Topology}
	
	We have not yet resolved on axis of which side of triangle should lie the point S. To address this question in a universal way, we reformulate the problem on a representative domain that encodes all possible triangles up to similarity.

	We start by introducing the representative domain. To describe all triangles up to similarity transformations, we construct a representative domain in the plane such that each similarity class of triangles corresponds to exactly one point in this domain.
	Let $\mathcal{T}$ denote the set of all non-degenerate triangles in the plane. 
	Two triangles $T_1, T_2 \in \mathcal{T}$ are said to be similar if their corresponding 
	side lengths are proportional. That is, if $a_i \le b_i \le c_i$ are the ordered 
	side lengths of $T_i$ for $i = 1,2,3$, then
	\[
	T_1 \sim T_2 
	\quad \text{if and only if} \quad
	\frac{a_1}{c_1} = \frac{a_2}{c_2}
	\quad \text{and} \quad
	\frac{b_1}{c_1} = \frac{b_2}{c_2}.
	\]
	This relation defines an equivalence relation on $\mathcal{T}$, and each 
	equivalence class represents one triangle shape.
	
	To obtain a unique representative of each similarity class, we fix two vertices
	\[
	A = (-0.5,0), \qquad B = (0.5,0),
	\]
	so that the side $AB$ has unit length and lies on the $x$-axis. 
	The third vertex is denoted by $C = (x,y)$.
	
	Since $AB$ is chosen as the longest side of the triangle, the remaining sides 
	must satisfy
	\[
	|AC| \le 1, \qquad |BC| \le 1.
	\]
	In Cartesian coordinates this implies the conditions
	\[
	\left(x+\frac12\right)^2+y^2 \le 1,
	\qquad
	\left(x-\frac12\right)^2+y^2 \le 1.
	\]
	
	To avoid counting mirror images, we restrict to the upper half-plane
	\[
	y > 0.
	\]
	Furthermore, interchanging the vertices $A$ and $B$ reflects the point $C$ across 
	the line $x = 0$. To ensure uniqueness of the representation, 
	we therefore impose the additional condition
	\[
	x\le0.
	\]
	\begin{definition}\label{def:domain}
	We thus define the representative domain
	\[
	\Omega =
	\left\{
	(x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 :
	y > 0,\;
	\left(x+\frac12\right)^2 + y^2 \le 1,\;
	\left(x-\frac12\right)^2 + y^2 \le 1,\;
	x \le 0
	\right\}.
	\]
	
	Each point $C \in \Omega$ uniquely determines a triangle
	\[
	T(C) = \{(-0.5,0), (0.5,0), (x,y)\},
	\]
	and every triangle in $\mathcal{T}$ is similar to exactly one such triangle. 
	Therefore, the domain $\Omega$ provides a complete and non-redundant 
	parametrization of all triangle shapes.
	\end{definition}
	We analyze, over the representative domain, which of the three possible
	topologies yields the minimal total price. Since we work with a representative
	domain, every triangle (up to similarity) can be transformed into this domain.
	Therefore, once the optimal topology is identified within the domain, we can
	determine the minimal relaxed LED tree for any given set of three leaves.
	
	In this section, we work with the function \(f\) representing the total price of the tree. It is function considered in area \(R_3\), for which it is
	possible to determine minimal positions of \(S,R\) without relaxation. In other words, we assume that the tree is minimal. Since the tree is stretched, the total price is given by the sum of the Euclidean lengths of it's edges.
	In following propositions we will work with \(f\):
	\begin{definition}\label{def:f}
		Let \(A = \left(-\tfrac12,0\right)\) and \(B = \left(\tfrac12,0\right)\) be fixed.
		We define the function \(f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}\) by
		\[
		f(C) := 2|AS| + |CS|,
		\]
		where \(S\) denotes point corresponding to the point \(C\) in minimal tree.
	\end{definition}
	We consider the function \(f\) in the plane containing the representative
	domain, with fixed vertices \(A\) and \(B\), while the point \(C\) is allowed
	to vary in the entire plane.
	Furthermore we define functions \(f_1, f_2\) that will consider 2 different topologies
	\begin{definition}\label{def:f1}
		Let \(f_1 : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}\) be the function defined as in
		\cref{def:f}, under the additional assumption that the point \(S\)
		lies on the perpendicular bisector of the segment \(AC\).
		
		Equivalently, the tree has the topology with edges \((A,S),\ (C,S),\ (S,R),\ (B,R).\)
	\end{definition}
	\begin{definition}\label{def:f2}
		Let \(f_2 : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}\) be defined analogously to
		\cref{def:f1}, with the  point \(S\) lying on the perpendicular
		bisector of the segment \(CB\).
		
		In this case, the tree has the topology with edges \( (C,S),\ (B,S),\ (S,R),\ (A,R), \)
	\end{definition}

	In the following lines, we show that the functions \(f, f_1, f_2\) are convex, this property will be useful later. 
	\begin{definition}\label{def:convex_function}
		Let \(D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n\) be a convex set. A function
		\(g : D \to \mathbb{R}\) is called convex if for all \(x_1,x_2 \in D\)
		and all \(\lambda \in [0,1]\), we have
		\[
		g(\lambda x_1 + (1-\lambda)x_2)
		\leq
		\lambda g(x_1) + (1-\lambda)g(x_2).
		\]
	\end{definition}
	First, we show that any function of the form of, sum of edges, is convex. We then show that the minimal function of this form is also convex.
	
	\begin{lemma}\label{lem:Fconvex}
		Let \(A,B \in \mathbb{R}^2\) be fixed points. Define the function
		\[
		F : \mathbb{R}^2 \times \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}
		\]
		by
		\[
		F(S,C) := |AS| + |SB| + |SC|.
		\]
		Then \(F\) is convex function of the variables \((S,C)\).
	\end{lemma}
	
	\begin{proof}
		We write
		\[
		F(S,C)=F_1(S)+F_2(S,C),
		\]
		where
		\[
		F_1(S):=|AS|+|SB|,
		\qquad
		F_2(S,C):=|SC|=\|C-S\|.
		\]
		The function \(F_1\) is convex, since it is the sum of two Euclidean distance
		functions with fixed endpoints.

		For the function \(F_2\), let \(S_1,S_2,C_1,C_2\in\mathbb{R}^2\) and
		\(\lambda\in[0,1]\). Put
		\[
		\widetilde S=\lambda S_1+(1-\lambda)S_2,
		\qquad
		\widetilde C=\lambda C_1+(1-\lambda)C_2.
		\]
		Then
		\[
		F_2(\widetilde S,\widetilde C)
		=
		\|\widetilde C-\widetilde S\|.
		\]
		Since
		\[
		\widetilde C-\widetilde S
		=
		\lambda(C_1-S_1)+(1-\lambda)(C_2-S_2),
		\]
		we obtain, by the convexity of the Euclidean norm,
		\[
		\begin{aligned}
			F_2(\widetilde S,\widetilde C)
			&=
			\|\lambda(C_1-S_1)+(1-\lambda)(C_2-S_2)\| \\
			&\leq
			\lambda\|C_1-S_1\|+(1-\lambda)\|C_2-S_2\| \\
			&=
			\lambda F_2(S_1,C_1)+(1-\lambda)F_2(S_2,C_2).
		\end{aligned}
		\]
		Thus \(F_2\) is convex and since \(F\) is the sum of the convex functions \(F_1\) and \(F_2\), it follows
		that \(F\) is convex.
	\end{proof}

	\begin{proposition}\label{prop:f_convex}
		Let \(f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}\) be function for minimal price defined by
		\[
		f(C) := |AS| + |SB| + |SC|,
		\]
		where S is chosen such that the expression attains its minimum.
		Then \(f\) is convex.
	\end{proposition}
	\begin{proof}
		Let \(C_1, C_2 \in \mathbb{R}^2\) and let \(S_1, S_2\) be corresponding
		minimizers, i.e.,
		\[
		f(C_1) = F(S_1, C_1), \qquad f(C_2) = F(S_2, C_2).
		\]
		
		Let \(\lambda \in [0,1]\) and define
		\[
		\widetilde C = \lambda C_1 + (1-\lambda) C_2,
		\qquad
		\widetilde S = \lambda S_1 + (1-\lambda) S_2.
		\]
		
		By definition of \(f\) as a minimum over all \(S\), we have
		\[
		f(\widetilde C)
		\leq
		F(\widetilde S, \widetilde C).
		\]
		
		Since \(F\) is convex by \cref{lem:Fconvex}, it follows that
		\[
		F(\widetilde S, \widetilde C)
		\leq
		\lambda F(S_1,C_1) + (1-\lambda)F(S_2,C_2).
		\]
		
		Combining these inequalities, we obtain
		\[
		f(\widetilde C)
		\leq
		\lambda f(C_1) + (1-\lambda)f(C_2).
		\]
		
		Therefore, \(f\) is convex.
	\end{proof}

	\begin{corollary}\label{cor:f1f2_convex}
		The functions \(f_1\) and \(f_2\) are convex.
	\end{corollary}
	\begin{proof}
		The proof follows analogously to \cref{prop:f_convex}, since both functions
		\(f_1\) and \(f_2\) are defined same as \(f\), to prescribed topology.
	\end{proof}


	\begin{lemma}\label{lem:f1_level_normal}
		Let \(C \in \Omega\), and let
		\[
		L_C := \{X \in \Omega : f_1(X)=f_1(C)\}
		\]
		denote the level curve of \(f_1\) passing through the point \(C\).
		Furthermore, let \(S = S(C)\) be the point corresponding
		to \(f_1(C)\).
		Then the vector
		\[
		CS^0 := \frac{CS}{|CS|}
		\]
		is the inward normal vector to the level curve \(L_C\).
	\end{lemma}
	\begin{proof}
		Let \(v\) be a tangent vector to the level curve \(L_C\).
		This by definition means that directional derivative of \(f_1\) in direction of vector \(v\),
		\[
		D_v f_1(C)=0.
		\]
		
		Also since we assume that point \(S\) lies on axis of AC, it follows that
		\[
		f_1(C)=2|AS|+|BS|,
		\]   
		where the point \(S=S(C)\) depends on the position of \(C\).
		
		Differentiating in the direction \(v\), we obtain
		\begin{align}\label{eq:kolmost}
		0 = D_v f_1(C) = (2AS^0+BS^0)\cdot \frac{dS}{dv}.
		\end{align}
		Hence,
		\[
		2AS^0+BS^0 \perp \frac{dS}{dv}.
		\]
		We have previously mentioned analogy to Snell's law  in form:	
		\[
		2 \sin \theta_1 = \sin \theta_2.
		\] 
		where \(\theta_1\) denotes the angle between the segment \(AS\) and the normal to \(AC\) axis, and \(\theta_2\) denotes the angle between the segment \(BS\) and the same normal to \(AC\) axis.  These angles are illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:Snell}).
		
		Now let \(n_{AC}\) denote a unit normal vector to the side \(AC\). Furthermore, let \(\alpha\) be the angle between \(n_{AC}\) and the segment \(AS\), and let \(\beta\) be the angle between the segment \(BS\) and \(n_{AC}\). Since  \(\alpha\), \(\beta\) are complementary to \(\theta_1\),\(\theta_2\) 
		\[
		\alpha=\frac{\pi}{2}-\theta_1
		\qquad
		\beta=\frac{\pi}{2}-\theta_2
		\]
		therefore
		\[
		2\cos\alpha=\cos\beta
		\]
		Furthermore, since for two vectors \(u,v \in \mathbb{R}^2\),
		the angle \(\theta\) between them satisfies
		\[
		u\cdot v = |u|\,|v|\,\cos\theta
		\]
		where both \(AS^0\) and \(n_{AC}\) are unit vectors, we obtain
		\[
		AS^0 \cdot n_{AC} = \cos\alpha
		\]
		Similarly,
		\[
		BS^0 \cdot n_{AC} = -\cos\beta
		\]
		than
		\begin{align*}
		2\cos\alpha&=\cos\beta \\
		2(AS^0\cdot n_{AC}) &=- BS^0\cdot n_{AC} \\
		(2AS^0+BS^0)\cdot n_{AC} &=0 \\
		\end{align*}
	
	Consequently, if the point \(C\) moves along the level curve of \(f_1\),
	the Steiner point \(S\) moves in a direction parallel to \(n_{AC}\).
		
		Function \(f_1(C)\) can also be written as
		\[
		f_1(C)=|AS|+|BS|+|CS|.
		\]
		Differentiating in the direction \(v\), we obtain
		\[
		0
		=
		D_v f_1(C)
		=
		(AS^0+BS^0+CS^0)\cdot\frac{dS}{dv}
		+
		CS^0\cdot v.
		\]
	From equation \ref{eq:kolmost} we know that first term of expression is equal to zero. Therefore the second term is equal to zero as well. 
	 	\[
	 	CS^0\cdot v=0.
	 	\]
	Thus \(CS^0\) is perpendicular to every tangent vector \(v\) of the level curve
	\(L_C\). In other words \(CS^0\) is a normal vector to \(L_C\).
	
	We know that level sets of \(f_1\) are convex and the function attains its minimum near the point \(A\), the
	vector \(CS^0\) points towards the interior of the level set. Hence, it is the inward normal vector.
	\end{proof}
	
	\begin{lemma}\label{lem:f2_normal}
		An analogous statement holds for the function \(f_2\). In particular, if
		\(L_C^{(2)} := \{X \in \Omega : f_2(X)=f_2(C)\}\) denotes the level curve
		of \(f_2\) passing through \(C\), then the unit vector
		\[
		CS_2^0 := \frac{S_2 - C}{|S_2 - C|}
		\]
		is the inward normal vector to \(L_C^{(2)}\).
	\end{lemma}
	
	\begin{proof}
		The proof follows analogously to Lemma \ref{lem:f1_level_normal}. Indeed, the
		function \(f_2\) is obtained from \(f_1\) by symmetry with respect to the
		\(y\)-axis, and the corresponding minimal tree has the same geometric
		properties. Therefore, the same argument applies.
	\end{proof}

	\begin{proposition}\label{prop:f1_leq_f2}
		For every \(C\in\Omega\), we have
		\[
		f_1(C)\leq f_2(C).
		\]
	\end{proposition}
	\begin{proof}
		Let \(C\in\Omega\). We move from \(C\) along the level curve of \(f_1\) in the
		tangent direction \(v\) chosen so that \(|AC|\) increases and \(|BC|\)
		decreases. Along this motion, the value of \(f_1\) remains constant.
		
		By \cref{lem:f1_level_normal}, the vector \(CS_1^0\) is the inward normal to
		the level curve of \(f_1\). Analogously, \(CS_2^0\) is the inward normal to the
		level curve of \(f_2\). From the geometry of the domain \(\Omega\), the chosen
		direction \(v\) forms an acute angle with \(CS_2^0\). Hence, moving in the
		direction \(v\) decreases the value of \(f_2\).
		
		We continue this motion until either
		\[
		|AC|+|BC|=|AB|
		\quad \text{or} \quad
		|AC|=|BC|.
		\]
		At such a point \(C^\ast\), the inequality \(f_1(C^\ast)\leq f_2(C^\ast)\)
		follows from the degenerate or symmetric configuration.
		
		Since \(f_1\) remains constant along the motion and \(f_2\) is strictly
		decreasing, we have
		\[
		f_2(C) \ge f_2(C^\ast).
		\]
		Combining this with \(f_1(C)=f_1(C^\ast)\), we obtain
		\[
		f_1(C)=f_1(C^\ast)\le f_2(C^\ast)\le f_2(C),
		\]
		which implies
		\[
		f_1(C)\le f_2(C).
		\]
	\end{proof}
	
	We have shown that \(f_1(C) \le f_2(C)\) for every point \(C \in \Omega\).
	However, this comparison involves only two of the possible expressions for the
	total price of a minimal relaxed LED tree.
	
	Recall Figure~\ref{fig:Careas} and the corresponding propositions. In that
	setting, depending on the position of the third point, the total price is
	described by one of the functions \(f\), \(g\), or \(h\).
	
	We now explain how this classification applies in the representative domain
	\(\Omega\). The argument is most naturally understood geometrically.
	
	Consider placing the diagram from Figure~\ref{fig:Careas} at a point
	\(C \in \Omega\). If we align the configuration so that it corresponds to
	the topology defining \(f_1\), that is, the symmetry axis is aligned with the
	perpendicular bisector of the side \(AC\), then the remaining vertex \(B\)
	always lies in the region \(R_3\). Consequently, only the function \(f\)
	is relevant in this case, and the value of \(f_1(C)\) coincides with the
	minimal price.
	
	On the other hand, if we orient the diagram so that it corresponds to the
	topology defining \(f_2\), that is, with the symmetry axis aligned with the
	perpendicular bisector of the side \(BC\), then the remaining vertex \(A\)
	lies predominantly in the region \(R_3\), but in certain configurations it
	may also lie in the region \(R_2\). In those cases, the total price is given
	by the function \(g\), which is larger than \(f\).
	
	Therefore, the value corresponding to the topology \(f_2\) is never smaller
	than the value corresponding to \(f_1\).
	\begin{proposition}\label{prop:PabPbc}
		For all \(C \in \Omega\), we have
		\[
		P_{AB}(C) \le P_{BC}(C),
		\]
		where \(P_{AB} = f_1\) and \(P_{BC} > f_2\).
	\end{proposition}

	The third possible configuration corresponds to the case where the point \(S\)
	lies on the perpendicular bisector of the side \(AB\), that is, on the
	\(y\)-axis.
	
	In this case, for points \(C \in \Omega\), the third vertex lies in the regions
	\(R_1\) or \(R_2\), and the total price is given by the function
	\[
	g(C) = 3|AS|.
	\]
	In such a configuration, all edges of the tree have equal length.
	
	This implies that the corresponding triangle is equilateral in the sense that
	the perpendicular bisectors of its sides intersect at a single point.
	Consequently, the point \(S\) also lies on the perpendicular bisector of the
	side \(AC\).
	
	However, we have already shown that among all configurations where \(S\) lies
	on the perpendicular bisector of \(AC\), the minimal price is given by the
	function \(f_1\). Therefore, the configuration corresponding to \(g\) cannot
	yield a smaller value than \(f_1\).
	\begin{proposition}
		For all \(C \in \Omega\), we have
		\[
		P_{AC}(C) \le P_{AB}(C),
		\]
		where \(P_{AC} = f_1\) and \(P_{AB} = g\).
	\end{proposition}

	Combining this result with \cref{prop:PabPbc}, we conclude that for all
	\(C \in \Omega\),
	\[
	f_1(C) \le f_2(C)
	\quad \text{and} \quad
	f_1(C) \le g(C).
	\]
	
	Therefore, in our configuration, the topology that has minimal total price
	is the one in which the Steiner point \(S\) lies on the perpendicular bisector
	of the segment \(AC\). In other words, this topology has edges
	\[
	(A,S),\ (C,S),\ (S,R),\ (R,B),
	\]
	and corresponds to the function \(f_1\).
	
	In particular, the minimal total price for this topology is given by
	\[
	\Ptotal{C} = f(C).
	\]
	
	We have shown that the topology yielding the minimal total price is the one
	in which the Steiner point \(S\) lies on the perpendicular bisector of the
	segment \(AC\).
	
	Since we work in the representative domain, this implies that in any triangle,
	the point \(S\) lies on the perpendicular bisector of the shortest side of the
	triangle formed by the leaves.
	
	Consequently, all geometric relations required for the construction of the
	minimal relaxed LED tree on three leaves are explicitly determined.
	
	\begin{proposition}\label{grandfinal}
		In a minimal relaxed LED tree on three leaves, the Steiner point \(S\) lies
		on the perpendicular bisector of the shortest side of the triangle.
		
		The minimal total price \(\Ptotal{T} \) is given by sum of Euclidean lengths of its edges.
		
	\end{proposition}

		\cos(\angle ACB) &= \frac{AC \cdot BC}{|AC||BC|}\\
		\cos(120^\circ) &=	\frac{x^2 - x + y^2}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\,\sqrt{(1-x)^2 + y^2}}\\
		-\frac{1}{2} &= \frac{x^2 - x + y^2}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\,\sqrt{(1-x)^2 + y^2}}
	\end{align*}
	This equation implicitly defines a circular arc, which can be derived more conveniently using geometric arguments. 
	
	We use a geometric argument about chord of a circle. For the set of points on the arc subtended by a chord, it holds that the angle formed with the endpoints of the chord is constant. In our scenario, set of points $C$ from which the segment $AB$ is seen under a constant angle $120^\circ$ forms an arc of a circle.
	The length of the segment is $|AB| = 1$, and using the chord formula,
	\[
	|AB| = 2r \sin(120^\circ),
	\]
	where \(r\) is radius of the circle, we obtain 
	\[
	1 = 2r \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2},
	\]
	which gives
	\[
	r = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}.
	\]
	
	We also know that the center of the circle lies on the axis of $AB$, i.e., on the line \(x = \frac{1}{2}\), therefore let the center be
	\[
	O = \left(\frac{1}{2}, k\right)
	\]
	Since the distance from $O$ to $A$ equals \(r = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}.\), we obtain \(k\) using Pythagoras's theorem
	\begin{align*}
	\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 + k^2 &= \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\right)^2\\
	k &= \pm \frac{1}{2\sqrt{3}}
	\end{align*}
	For the upper arc corresponding to the $120^\circ$ condition, the center lies below the $x$-axis, hence
	\[
	k = -\frac{1}{2\sqrt{3}}.
	\]
	Therefore, the center is
	\[
	O = \left(\frac{1}{2}, -\frac{1}{2\sqrt{3}}\right),
	\]
	From which we obtain the equation of the circle
	\[
	\left(x - \frac{1}{2}\right)^2 + \left(y + \frac{1}{2\sqrt{3}}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{3}.
	\]
	Solving for $y$, we obtain explicit equation of the boundary curve
	\[
	y = \sqrt{\frac{1}{3} - \left(x - \frac{1}{2}\right)^2} - \frac{1}{2\sqrt{3}}.
	\]
	
	This curve separates the domain into two regions:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item below the curve, where $\angle ACB \geq 120^\circ$ and the Steiner point coincides with $C$, (red region) 
		\item above the curve, where $\angle ACB < 120^\circ$ and a nontrivial Steiner point exists inside the  triangle (blue region)
	\end{itemize}

		\includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{figures/boundary120_region.pdf}
		\caption{Subdivision of the computational domain according to the angle condition.}
		\label{fig:boundary120_region}
	\end{figure}
	We will use this separation when computing minimal Steiner point, tree length. 
	Let us define a function $L(C)$, which assigns to each point $C$ in the domain the total length of the minimal Steiner tree. It is defined as the sum of Euclidean lengths of all edges in the tree.
	More precisely, we define
	\[
	L(C) =
	\begin{cases}
		|AC| + |BC|, & \text{if } \angle ACB \geq 120^\circ, \\[0.5em]
		|AS| + |BS| + |CS|, & \text{if } \angle ACB < 120^\circ,
	\end{cases}
	\]
	For second case, the solution is straight forward but in order to evaluate the function $L(C)$, in first case. it is necessary to determine the position of the minimal Steiner point $S$
	
	For this purpose, we use an iterative numerical method proposed by mathematician called Smith. It provides algorithm for computing minimal Steiner points in a given topology. This algorithm has it's importance and it is worth mentioning, because it provides numerical solution for arbitrary configuration.  
	
	The algorithm constructs a sequence of approximations of the minimal Steiner point, which converges to the optimal solution. We do not reproduce the proof of convergence here, as it can be found in the referenced literature.
	\begin{algorithm}[H]
		\caption{Smith's Numerical Method}
		\label{alg:smith}
		Let $T$ be a Steiner topology with $n$ terminals and $k$ Steiner points.  
		Let $s_k = (x_k, y_k)$ denote the $k$-th Steiner point.  
		Let the coordinates of points adjacent to $s_k$ be denoted by $w_j = (u_j, v_j)$, where $j$ ranges over all vertices such that $[w_j, s_k]$ is an edge of the tree $T$.
		
		At iteration step $i = 0,1,2,\dots$, the coordinates of each Steiner point are updated by solving the system of linear equations
		\begin{align}
			x_k^{(i+1)} &= 
			\frac{\displaystyle \sum_j \frac{u^{i+1}_j}{\sqrt{(u^{i}_j - x_k^{i})^2 + (v^{i}_j - y_k^{i})^2}}}
			{\displaystyle \sum_j \frac{1}{\sqrt{(u^{i}_j - x_k^{i})^2 + (v^{i}_j - y_k^{i})^2}}}, \\[0.8em]
			y_k^{(i+1)} &= 
			\frac{\displaystyle \sum_j \frac{v^{i+1}_j}{\sqrt{(u^{i}_j - x_k^{i})^2 + (v^{i}_j - y_k^{i})^2}}}
			{\displaystyle \sum_j \frac{1}{\sqrt{(u^{i}_j - x_k^{i})^2 + (v^{i}_j - y_k^{i})^2}}}.
		\end{align}
	\end{algorithm}

	In our case, we consider a tree with three terminals $A$, $B$, and $C$, and a single Steiner point $S = (S_1, S_2)$. The iterative scheme simplifies to the following:
	\begin{align}
		S^{i+1}_1 &=
		\frac{
			\frac{1}{\sqrt{(1 - S^{i}_1)^2 + (S^{i}_2)^2}}
			+
			\frac{C_x}{\sqrt{(C_x - S^{i}_1)^2 + (C_y - S^{i}_2)^2}}
		}{
			\frac{1}{\sqrt{(S^{i}_1)^2 + (S^{i}_2)^2}}
			+
			\frac{1}{\sqrt{(1 - S^{i}_1)^2 + (S^{i}_2)^2}}
			+
			\frac{1}{\sqrt{(C_x - S^{i}_1)^2 + (C_y - S^{i}_2)^2}}
		}, \\[0.7em]
		S^{i+1}_2 &=
		\frac{
			\frac{C_y}{\sqrt{(C_x - S^{i}_1)^2 + (C_y - S^{i}_2)^2}}
		}{
			\frac{1}{\sqrt{(S^{i}_1)^2 + (S^{i}_2)^2}}
			+
			\frac{1}{\sqrt{(1 - S^{i}_1)^2 + (S^{i}_2)^2}}
			+
			\frac{1}{\sqrt{(C_x - S^{i}_1)^2 + (C_y - S^{i}_2)^2}}
		}.
	\end{align}
		Therefore, in each iteration, only two equations need to be solved.	In practice, the iteration converges rapidly and only a small number of steps is required to achieve sufficient accuracy.

	\chapter{Conclusions}
	
	In this thesis, we studied optimization trees related to the Euclidean Steiner problem and LED trees. The work first introduced the necessary notions from graph theory, weighted graphs, Euclidean graphs, and rooted trees. Afterwards, we summarized the basic theory of Steiner minimal trees, including their geometric properties and the classical Fermat problem for three terminals.
	
	The main focus of the thesis was the study of minimal relaxed LED trees in the case of three leaves. We introduced the notion of relaxed LED trees and investigated their geometric behavior. In particular, we described the structure of minimal configurations and presented a constructive procedure for obtaining minimal relaxed LED trees in the three-leaf case.
	
	An important part of the thesis was the comparison between Steiner minimal trees and relaxed LED trees. Although both problems are based on similar combinatorial structures, the obtained geometric properties differ in several aspects. The comparison showed both similarities and fundamental differences between these two optimization models.
	
	The obtained results suggest several possible directions for further research. One natural continuation would be the study of relaxed LED trees with a larger number of leaves and the investigation of their structural properties. Another possible direction is the development of algorithms for constructing minimal relaxed LED trees or the study of these problems in higher-dimensional Euclidean spaces.
